• "White Dwarf"

    Definition: A white dwarf is a stellar remnant composed mostly of electron-degenerate matter. It is the final evolutionary state of stars not massive enough to become neutron stars or black holes1. Density: White dwarfs are incredibly dense. Their mass is comparable to the Sun’s, but their volume is similar to Earth’s1. Luminosity: They have low luminosity, emitting residual thermal energy since no fusion occurs within them1. Temperature: White dwarfs have surface temperatures ranging from 8,000 to 40,000 K2. Lifespan: They can exist for billions of years, gradually cooling over time2. Examples: Notable white dwarfs include Sirius B and Procyon B2. Formation: They form when stars with initial masses up to about 8 times that of the Sun exhaust their nuclear fuel and shed their outer layers3. Support Mechanism: White dwarfs are supported against gravitational collapse by electron degeneracy pressure1

  • "Red Dwarf"

    Definition: Red dwarf stars are the smallest and coolest stars on the main sequence, typically of spectral class M or K1. Prevalence: They are the most common type of star in the Milky Way galaxy, making up about 73% of all stars1. Temperature: Red dwarfs have surface temperatures ranging from 2,000 to 4,000 K2. Luminosity: They are much dimmer than the Sun, with luminosities ranging from 0.0001 to 0.8 times that of the Sun1. Lifespan: Red dwarfs have incredibly long lifespans, potentially lasting trillions of years due to their slow hydrogen fusion process2. Mass: They typically have masses between 0.08 and 0.6 times that of the Sun3. Examples: Notable red dwarfs include Proxima Centauri, the closest star to the Sun, and TRAPPIST-12. Formation: They form from clouds of dust and gas pulled together by gravity, similar to other main-sequence stars2. Fusion Process: Red dwarfs burn hydrogen both in their cores and throughout their entire structure, which contributes to their extended lifetimes

  • "Neutron Star"

    Definition: Neutron stars are the collapsed cores of massive supergiant stars, formed after a supernova explosion1. Density: They are incredibly dense; a sugar-cube-sized amount of neutron star material would weigh about a billion tons on Earth1. Size: Typically, neutron stars have a radius of about 10 kilometers (6 miles)1. Mass: They usually have a mass between 1.4 and 2.1 times that of the Sun1. Temperature: Newly formed neutron stars can have surface temperatures of around 1 million K, cooling over time1. Rotation: Neutron stars can rotate extremely rapidly, up to several hundred times per second2. Magnetic Fields: They possess extremely strong magnetic fields, which can be billions of times stronger than Earth’s2. Types: There are different types of neutron stars, including pulsars and magnetars2. Examples: Notable neutron stars include the Crab Pulsar and PSR J0952-0607, the most massive neutron star detected so far

  • "Brown Dwarf"

    Definition: Brown dwarfs are substellar objects that are more massive than the largest gas giant planets but less massive than the smallest stars1. Formation: They form like stars through the gravitational collapse of gas clouds but do not gain enough mass to sustain hydrogen fusion2. Mass Range: Typically between 13 and 80 times the mass of Jupiter1. Temperature: Surface temperatures range from about 1,500 to 2,800 degrees Fahrenheit (830 to 1,500 degrees Celsius)3. Recent Discovery: The James Webb Space Telescope recently identified the smallest known free-floating brown dwarf, with only three to four times the mass of Jupiter34. Location: Brown dwarfs can be found in various star-forming regions, such as the IC 348 cluster in the Perseus constellation4. Observation: They emit most of their light in the infrared spectrum, making them detectable with infrared telescopes1.

  • "Protostar"

    Definition: A protostar is a very young star that is still gathering mass from its parent molecular cloud1. Formation: Protostars form when a dense region within a molecular cloud collapses under its own gravity12. Duration: For low-mass stars (like the Sun), the protostar phase lasts about 500,000 years1. Temperature: Protostars have lower temperatures compared to main-sequence stars3. Evolution: The phase ends when the infalling gas is depleted, leaving a pre-main-sequence star1. Observation: Protostars are often observed in regions with high concentrations of gas and dust, such as the Orion Nebula2.
